What a chain-of-thought monitor actually detects

Notes on a small CoT-injection study (H1–H2). Subject: DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B. Monitor: Claude Haiku 4.5. Dataset: 50 MMLU items. A headline result that rests on a calibration point I'd want others to check before trusting it.

The threat model

CoT oversight assumes that reading a model's reasoning tells you something true about how it reached its answer. AI-control proposals build on this — box an untrusted model, have a trusted monitor watch its chain-of-thought, and flag anything that looks like the untrusted model pursuing a bad objective. The assumption breaks if an attacker can write into the CoT.

Closed models cryptographically sign their thinking blocks, so the realistic attack surface is open-weight reasoning models, where the <think> block is just text the harness controls. The question I asked: if an attacker controls part of that trace, how robust is CoT monitoring, and how does that robustness scale with how much of the trace the attacker owns?

I framed it as a standard control eval — an untrusted subject (a 1.5B distilled reasoner, chosen precisely because its think block is injectable), an attack policy and a blind monitor (both Claude Haiku 4.5), a faithfulness judge, and an attack-cost axis (injection length).

H1: length

I pre-registered the hypothesis that monitorability degrades non-linearly as the injection grows — more attacker tokens, more room to bury the tell, lower signal for the monitor. It was falsified. The monitor flagged more as injections got longer (flag rate roughly 65% → 75% → 93% across 100/300/700 tokens), not less.

The result that mattered came from the validity audit, not the hypothesis. Two findings reshaped everything. First, behavioral influence is real and scales: after a clean-baseline filter — counting only items the model did not already answer the attacker's way unprompted — the injection flipped the answer 23% → 31% → 61% of the time as length grew, and the model essentially never acknowledged the planted reasoning.

Second, and more uncomfortable: the "strong" monitor flagged 42% of perfectly clean, un-injected traces. Its 93% flag rate at 700 tokens is not 93% detection; it is a monitor flagging most of what it reads. Reporting flag rate without false-positive rate would have been the central error of the study.

Correcting for that with Youden's J (flag rate minus false-positive rate), the capable monitor's real informedness rises with length, while a small Qwen monitor's falls, and a 0.5B monitor carries no signal at all. So "does a longer injection get caught more?" has no answer independent of which monitor you ask. There is no single monitorability-versus-length curve — there is one per capability tier, and they point in opposite directions.

H2: content vs. verbosity

H1 left an obvious confound: was the monitor reacting to bad reasoning, or merely to length and anomaly? H2 tests this with matched pairs. For each successful coherent-wrong injection (Family B), I generated an off-topic filler injection (Family C) matched in length and final answer letter, but with no on-topic content. The only thing that varies is content quality.

The pre-registered metric — the gap in undetected-success rate between B and C — came out at zero. But that is the same 42% false-positive artifact again, not a real null. The signal is in separating influence from detection. At 700 tokens, the coherent argument captured the answer 61% of the time against 12% for filler: content drove a 5× difference in how often the attack actually worked. Yet the monitor flagged both at essentially the same rate (92% vs 91%). A 61%-effective attack and a 12%-effective one look identical to it.

At 100 tokens, content didn't matter for influence either, and the monitor actually flagged the off-topic filler more — the opposite of what I'd predicted.

The conclusion the data supports is narrow but sharp: this monitor is a length-and-anomaly detector, not a reasoning-quality detector. Its flag tells you that something unusual is in the trace. It tells you nothing about whether the injection worked.

Why I think this matters

Put H1 and H2 together and the picture is consistent: length drives both influence and flagging; content drives only influence. The monitoring signal is, functionally, a length detector sitting on top of a real length-by-content effect on behaviour.

For anyone leaning on CoT monitoring for oversight, two things follow. A monitor that flags everything looks maximally safe — near-zero successful evasion — while being useless, because it has no discriminative power. And the apparent robustness here was entirely an artifact of miscalibration: against a properly calibrated monitor, the same data implies substantial undetected influence, and the content effect reappears.

Recall without calibration is not evidence of safety, and an evasion metric computed against a trigger-happy monitor measures the monitor's base rate, not the attack.

What the injection is doing inside the model

The monitoring results describe the trace from the outside. I also wanted to know what the injection does inside the model, so I used activation patching, swapping internal activations from the injected run into a clean one, on the 66 items where the clean answer differs from the target.

I checked first that patching the full residual reproduces the original effect, so the component patches are trustworthy. Patching the residual stream layer by layer, the override commits at layer 22, and it's written by attention rather than the MLP. Broken down by head, three heads at layer 22 carry most of it (heads 7, 4, and 9), with one other head pushing the answer the other way. They also transfer: take those heads' outputs from one question and write them into a different one, and the second question moves to the answer I picked in 40% of the pairs I tried. So the override is a small, reusable object rather than something smeared across the whole network.

Two things follow that bear on the monitoring question.

First, when the injection wins, does it erase the model's original answer or just outvote it? Reading each layer's belief in the target and the original answer with a logit lens, it comes out a mix. About +4.25 of the swing is the target being built up and +2.83 is the original being torn down, an overwrite share of 0.37, so both happen, with building-up the larger part.

Second, and this is the part that matters for monitoring. Because the override is just those three heads, you don't need the injected text at all. Patch their outputs into a clean run, with nothing added to the think block, and the answer still moves to the target in 26% of items. The trace a monitor reads is the original clean prefix, unchanged in every case, so a text monitor returns the same verdict whether or not the edit happened. This is partly definitional, since a text monitor obviously can't read activations. The point is that the thing needed to flip the answer is this small and this localized, and it leaves nothing in the text to catch. So the loud text attack from the first half of this post, the one the monitor flags about 92% of the time, has a quiet twin it never sees.

Caveats

This is one 1.5B subject, one attack generator, and one primary monitor and the generator and monitor share a model family, which is a real confound with small per-cell samples. The robust claims are the signs and orderings, not the magnitudes.
